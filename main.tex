\section{Introduction}
Spectral clustering is a graph-based clustering method that is especially attractive when cluster geometry is non-convex, curved, or better described by local neighborhood relations than by Euclidean centroids. The basic move is to convert data into a weighted graph, study the graph Laplacian, extract a small number of eigenvectors, and run a simple clustering method in that spectral embedding. We focus on the normalized spectral clustering algorithm of Ng, Jordan, and Weiss (NJW) \cite{ng2001spectral}, while relating it to the normalized-cut objective of Shi and Malik \cite{shi2000normalized} and the broader graph-Laplacian view in von Luxburg's tutorial \cite{vonluxburg2007tutorial}.

The report combines three complementary pieces: a compact theoretical account of normalized spectral clustering, a Python implementation with explicit computational analysis, and an empirical study across synthetic, vector, and graph datasets.

\section{Theory}
\subsection{Graph construction and Laplacians}
Given data points $x_1,\dots,x_n \in \mathbb{R}^d$, we build a weighted undirected graph with affinity matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$, where $W_{ij}$ is large when $x_i$ and $x_j$ are similar. Let $D$ be the diagonal degree matrix, $D_{ii} = \sum_j W_{ij}$. The two most common Laplacians are the unnormalized Laplacian
\[
L = D - W
\]
and the symmetric normalized Laplacian
\[
L_{\mathrm{sym}} = I - D^{-1/2} W D^{-1/2}.
\]
When the graph has $k$ connected components, $L_{\mathrm{sym}}$ has exactly $k$ zero eigenvalues, and the associated eigenvectors are piecewise constant on those components. In the nearly disconnected case, the bottom $k$ eigenvectors remain approximately piecewise constant, which is the spectral signal exploited by clustering algorithms \cite{vonluxburg2007tutorial}.

\subsection{From normalized cut to an eigenproblem}
For a two-way partition $(A, \bar A)$, the cut is
\[
\mathrm{cut}(A,\bar A) = \sum_{i\in A,\,j\in \bar A} W_{ij}.
\]
Minimizing cut alone is degenerate because it prefers isolating tiny sets. Shi and Malik proposed the normalized cut objective
\[
\mathrm{Ncut}(A,\bar A)
=
\frac{\mathrm{cut}(A,\bar A)}{\mathrm{vol}(A)}
+
\frac{\mathrm{cut}(A,\bar A)}{\mathrm{vol}(\bar A)},
\qquad
\mathrm{vol}(A)=\sum_{i\in A} D_{ii},
\]
which balances low inter-cluster connectivity against cluster size \cite{shi2000normalized}. The exact optimization is discrete and NP-hard in general, so spectral clustering uses a continuous relaxation. That relaxation leads to a generalized eigenvalue problem and, after normalization, to the bottom eigenvectors of a Laplacian matrix. This is the main mathematical reason spectral clustering works: difficult discrete partitioning is replaced by a tractable linear algebra problem \cite{shi2000normalized,vonluxburg2007tutorial}.

\subsection{Why NJW?}
There are several common flavors of spectral clustering. Unnormalized methods work directly with $L=D-W$. Shi-Malik normalized cuts solve a generalized eigenproblem. NJW instead computes the top eigenvectors of a normalized affinity-like matrix, or equivalently the bottom eigenvectors of a normalized Laplacian, then normalizes rows and applies $k$-means \cite{ng2001spectral}. We chose NJW because it is simple, standard, easy to implement in Python, and closely tied to the normalized-cut relaxation while remaining computationally clean.

\section{Algorithm}
\subsection{Implemented method}
Our implementation uses an $m$-nearest-neighbor graph with RBF weights:
\[
W_{ij} = \exp\!\bigl(-\gamma \lVert x_i-x_j\rVert_2^2\bigr)
\]
when $x_j$ is among the $m$ nearest neighbors of $x_i$. The scale parameter $\gamma$ is chosen from the median nonzero neighbor distance. The graph is then symmetrized.

\paragraph{Input.} Data matrix $X\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d}$, number of clusters $k$, number of neighbors $m$.

\paragraph{Output.} Predicted cluster labels $\hat y_1,\dots,\hat y_n$.

\paragraph{Steps.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em]
    \item Build a sparse weighted $m$-nearest-neighbor graph.
    \item Form $L_{\mathrm{sym}} = I-D^{-1/2}WD^{-1/2}$.
    \item Compute the $k$ eigenvectors associated with the smallest eigenvalues of $L_{\mathrm{sym}}$.
    \item Stack them into $U\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times k}$ and row-normalize to obtain $Y$.
    \item Run $k$-means on the rows of $Y$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Computational cost}
There are three main costs.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em]
    \item \textbf{Graph construction.} Exact pairwise distances would cost $O(n^2d)$, but the stored sparse $k$-NN graph has only $O(mn)$ nonzeros.
    \item \textbf{Eigensolve.} This is the main bottleneck. Dense eigendecomposition is cubic in $n$, while sparse iterative eigensolvers scale much better in practice and depend strongly on the number of graph edges and the spectral gap \cite{vonluxburg2007tutorial,ng2001spectral}.
    \item \textbf{$k$-means in spectral space.} Once the embedding is computed, the final step is cheap because the embedding has only $k$ columns.
\end{enumerate}

We measured these costs directly. On a two-moons benchmark, increasing $n$ from $150$ to $900$ increased the spectral pipeline runtime from about $0.012$ s to $0.220$ s, while the plain $k$-means baseline remained around $0.009$--$0.012$ s. The eigensolver dominated the growth, while graph memory rose from about $24$ KiB to $161$ KiB for the sparse affinity matrix. Figure~\ref{fig:scaling} shows these trends. Exact timings vary by machine, so we interpret the trend rather than the individual seconds.

\section{Experimental design}
\subsection{Evaluation metrics}
For labeled benchmarks we report accuracy after optimal label matching, adjusted Rand index (ARI), normalized mutual information (NMI), and purity.

Because cluster labels are permutation-invariant, accuracy is computed only after solving the best one-to-one assignment between predicted cluster IDs and ground-truth classes:
\[
\mathrm{Acc}(y,\hat y)=\frac{1}{n}\max_{\pi\in S_k}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}\{y_i = \pi(\hat y_i)\}.
\]
Here $S_k$ denotes the set of permutations of the cluster labels.

ARI compares two partitions through pairwise co-membership counts and adjusts for the agreement expected by chance. Using the standard contingency-table notation, it can be written as \cite{hubert1985comparing}
\[
\mathrm{ARI}=
\frac{
\sum_{ij} \binom{n_{ij}}{2} -
\dfrac{\sum_i \binom{a_i}{2} \sum_j \binom{b_j}{2}}{\binom{n}{2}}
}{
\dfrac{1}{2}\left[\sum_i \binom{a_i}{2}+\sum_j \binom{b_j}{2}\right]-
\dfrac{\sum_i \binom{a_i}{2} \sum_j \binom{b_j}{2}}{\binom{n}{2}}
}.
\]
A value near $0$ indicates roughly chance-level agreement, and $1$ indicates identical partitions.

NMI measures the information shared by two partitions:
\[
\mathrm{NMI}(U,V)=\frac{I(U;V)}{\mathrm{avg}\bigl(H(U),H(V)\bigr)},
\]
where $I(U;V)$ is mutual information and $H(\cdot)$ is entropy. The normalized version lies in $[0,1]$ and is invariant to label permutations \cite{sklearnNMI}.

Purity assigns each predicted cluster to its majority true label and reports the resulting fraction of correctly assigned points:
\[
\mathrm{Purity}(\mathcal{C},\mathcal{G}) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{C\in \mathcal{C}} \max_{G\in \mathcal{G}} |C \cap G|.
\]
In the discussion below, ARI is the most informative single metric because it is sensitive to global partition structure rather than only majority assignment.

\subsection{Datasets}
We used the following groups of experiments.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
    \item \textbf{Synthetic success cases:} two moons, concentric circles, Gaussian blobs, high-dimensional moons (moons embedded in 20 dimensions), and high-dimensional blobs.
    \item \textbf{Synthetic stress cases:} overlapping blobs and a bridge-connected two-cluster dataset.
    \item \textbf{Real/vector datasets:} Iris and four digit experiments: $\{0,1\}$, $\{1,7\}$, $\{0,1,3,6\}$, and all ten classes. We use \texttt{sklearn.datasets.load\_digits}, which provides a compact and reproducible benchmark \cite{sklearnDigits}.
    \item \textbf{Graph datasets:} Zachary's karate club and a stochastic block model benchmark.
    \item \textbf{Robustness and scaling:} a Gaussian-noise sweep on two moons, and a runtime/memory benchmark as $n$ increases.
\end{itemize}

For graph datasets, plain feature-space $k$-means is not naturally defined. To keep the comparison explicit, we used a weak but transparent baseline: $k$-means on adjacency rows. This is not a state-of-the-art graph clustering baseline, but it provides a concrete comparator.

The scatter plot should therefore be read as evidence of partial geometric recovery rather than near-perfect agreement with the reference labels.

\section{Results: real data and digit subsets}
We study four digit subsets of increasing difficulty: $\{0,1\}$, $\{1,7\}$, $\{0,1,3,6\}$, and all digits. This progression makes it possible to distinguish regimes where local graph structure is helpful from regimes where raw pixel-space geometry already favors centroid-based clustering. Table~\ref{tab:digits} reports those results. The outcome is deliberately presented as a mixed result rather than a blanket win for spectral clustering. Spectral clustering is excellent on $\{0,1\}$ and strong on all ten digits, but it is slightly worse than $k$-means on $\{1,7\}$ and clearly worse on $\{0,1,3,6\}$ for the particular $k$-NN graph used here. Our hypothesis is that raw pixel-space Euclidean distances already separate some digit subsets nearly linearly, while the local graph can become brittle when classes have different stroke styles and densities.

\section{Results: graph data}
Spectral clustering is especially natural on graph data. On Zachary's karate club graph, spectral clustering reaches accuracy $0.971$ and ARI $0.882$, far above the adjacency-row $k$-means baseline (accuracy $0.676$, ARI $0.109$). On a planted stochastic block model, spectral clustering perfectly recovers the communities, while adjacency-row $k$-means is also strong but slightly worse. These two experiments strengthen the community-detection side of the study.

\section{Additional experiments}

\subsection{Parameter sensitivity map}
Spectral clustering depends strongly on the graph it constructs. Figure~\ref{fig:paramsensitivity} shows a phase-diagram style sweep on two moons, varying both the feature noise and the number of nearest neighbors in the graph. The final red-outlined column reports the $k$-means baseline at the same noise level. The main pattern is useful: there is a broad stable band at moderate neighbor counts, but too few neighbors can fragment the graph, while too many neighbors can wash out the two-moon geometry once noise is substantial. In the high-neighbor regime, performance often approaches the $k$-means baseline, consistent with the idea that an overly dense neighborhood graph loses the local manifold structure that gives spectral clustering its advantage.

\subsection{Eigengap study}
A common heuristic for spectral clustering is that the first few eigenvalues of the graph Laplacian reveal the number and strength of clusters. Figure~\ref{fig:eigengap} compares the smallest eigenvalues on several datasets. The stochastic block model has a clear gap after the third eigenvalue and is perfectly recovered, which matches the textbook story. The two-moons case also has useful low-eigenvalue structure. But the overlapping blobs and digit cases are less clean: a visible gap alone is not a complete certificate of good clustering. This experiment makes the eigengap heuristic concrete while also showing its limitation.

\subsection{Graph construction ablation}
The strongest additional lesson is that graph construction is the main inductive bias. We compare three affinity constructions:
\[
W_{ij}^{\mathrm{dense}} = \exp(-\gamma \lVert x_i-x_j\rVert_2^2),
\]
\[
W_{ij}^{\mathrm{kNN}} = \mathbf{1}\{j\in \mathcal{N}_m(i) \text{ or } i\in \mathcal{N}_m(j)\} \exp(-\gamma \lVert x_i-x_j\rVert_2^2),
\]
and
\[
W_{ij}^{\mathrm{mutual}} = \mathbf{1}\{j\in \mathcal{N}_m(i) \text{ and } i\in \mathcal{N}_m(j)\} \exp(-\gamma \lVert x_i-x_j\rVert_2^2).
\]
Here $\mathcal{N}_m(i)$ denotes the directed $m$-nearest-neighbor set of point $i$. Figure~\ref{fig:graphablation} shows that the same spectral clustering pipeline can behave very differently depending on this choice. On moons and circles, local sparse graphs preserve manifold geometry and dense RBF does poorly. On the digit subset $\{0,1,3,6\}$, dense RBF performs well, while mutual-$k$-NN becomes too restrictive and loses useful edges. The conclusion is not that one graph is universally best; rather, graph construction must match the data geometry.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.70\textwidth]{final_pic/graph_construction_ablation.png}
    \caption{Graph construction ablation. The same spectral clustering pipeline can behave very differently depending on whether the graph is dense, local sparse, or mutual local.}
    \label{fig:graphablation}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Graph fragility archetypes}
Figure~\ref{fig:failuretaxonomy} collects three interpretable fragility modes. First, too few neighbors can fragment a manifold graph. Second, a long thin bridge of intermediate points can remove the clean low-density separation between two masses. Third, varying density and imbalance can make a single global neighborhood scale inappropriate. The point of this figure is not that spectral clustering always fails on such inputs; rather, it illustrates how fixed graph parameters can become mismatched to the local geometry of the data.

\section{Discussion}
Overall, the experiments match the standard theory quite well.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
    \item On non-convex synthetic datasets, spectral clustering substantially outperforms $k$-means.
    \item On standard blob-like data, both methods are strong and $k$-means can even be slightly better.
    \item On graph/community datasets, spectral clustering is especially natural and strong.
    \item On the digit subsets, spectral clustering is not uniformly better; it ties on $\{0,1\}$, is nearly perfect but slightly worse on $\{1,7\}$, loses on $\{0,1,3,6\}$, and wins clearly on all ten digits. This is a useful reminder that graph construction is a modeling choice, not a free lunch.
\end{itemize}

The main surprises are the $\{1,7\}$ and $\{0,1,3,6\}$ digit subsets, where $k$-means already aligns well with raw pixel geometry and our particular neighborhood graph is comparatively poor. The high-dimensional moons and bridge examples also show that a numerical advantage can be visually modest. These are informative negative results rather than embarrassments: they expose exactly where normalized spectral clustering depends on a good graph model.
