\section{Introduction and Motivation}
% =============================================================

Spectral clustering partitions data by exploiting the eigenstructure of a matrix derived from pairwise similarities. This report will focus on the clustering method contributed by Ng, Jordan, and Weiss(hereafter \textbf{NJW}).

\noindent 
The benefits of NJW clustering is threefold. First, they propose a clean, specific algorithm for $k$-way clustering that uses $k$ eigenvectors simultaneously rather than applying a two-way cut recursively. Second, they provide an analysis---using matrix perturbation theory---that gives precise conditions under which the algorithm is guaranteed to succeed. Third, they demonstrate that a seemingly small implementation detail---row-normalizing the eigenvector matrix before applying $k$-means---is in fact critical and has a deep geometric justification.

The NJW algorithm is closely related to the Normalized Cut (Ncut) of Shi and Malik~\cite{ShiMalik2000}, but differs in the Laplacian it employs. Whereas Shi and Malik work with the asymmetric random walk Laplacian $L_{\mathrm{rw}} = D^{-1}(D-W)$, NJW use the \textbf{symmetric normalized Laplacian} $L_{\mathrm{sym}} = D^{-1/2}(D - W)D^{-1/2}$. This difference has both numerical and theoretical consequences that we explore throughout this document.

% =============================================================
\section{Setup: The Affinity Graph}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Data and Affinity Matrix}

Let $S = \{s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_n\} \subset \mathbb{R}^l$ be the dataset. NJW represent pairwise relationships through an \textbf{affinity matrix} $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ defined by:
\begin{equation}
    A_{ij} =
    \begin{cases}
        \exp\!\left(-\dfrac{\|s_i - s_j\|^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) & \text{if } i \neq j, \\[6pt]
        0 & \text{if } i = j,
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:affinity}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma^2 > 0$ is a scale parameter that controls how quickly affinity decays with distance. NJW set self-affinities $A_{ii} = 0$ to prevent a vertex from dominating its own degree.

The matrix $A$ is symmetric and nonnegative. It plays the role of $W$ in the graph Laplacian literature. The parameter $\sigma^2$ is the primary hyperparameter of the method: a large $\sigma^2$ makes all pairs nearly equally similar, collapsing all cluster structure; a small $\sigma^2$ makes the graph sparse and sensitive to scale. NJW later show that $\sigma^2$ can be chosen automatically by searching for the value that produces the tightest clusters in the spectral embedding (see Section~\ref{sec:sigma}).

\subsection{Degree Matrix and Symmetric Normalized Laplacian}

Define the diagonal degree matrix $D$ with entries $D_{ii} = \sum_j A_{ij}$ (total affinity of vertex $i$ to all other vertices). NJW then construct the matrix:
\begin{equation}
    L = D^{-1/2} A D^{-1/2},
    \label{eq:L_njw}
\end{equation}
which is the \textbf{symmetric normalized affinity matrix}. Note that NJW define $L$ as the normalized affinity (not the Laplacian $I - L$), so in their notation the relevant eigenvectors are the \emph{largest} ones of $L$, corresponding to the \emph{smallest} eigenvalues of $L_{\mathrm{sym}} = I - L = D^{-1/2}(D - A)D^{-1/2}$.


The matrix $L = D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$ is symmetric and positive semidefinite. Its eigenvalues $1 = \lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_n \geq -1$ are real by the Perron-Frobenius Theorem.

\subsection{Relationship to the Random Walk Laplacian}

There is a close algebraic relationship between NJW's $L$ and the transition matrix $P = D^{-1}A$ of the natural random walk on the affinity graph, which we encountered during lectures. Specifically:
\begin{equation}
    L = D^{-1/2} A D^{-1/2} = D^{1/2} P D^{-1/2}.
\end{equation}
This is a similarity transformation: $L$ and $P$ have the same eigenvalues. If $\mathbf{v}$ is an eigenvector of $L$ with eigenvalue $\lambda$, then $\mathbf{u} = D^{-1/2}\mathbf{v}$ is an eigenvector of $P$ with the same eigenvalue $\lambda$. The NJW approach therefore encodes the same spectral information as the random walk, but in a symmetric form that is easier to work with analytically and numerically.

\section{The NJW Algorithm}
% =============================================================

\subsection{The Six Steps}

NJW propose the following algorithm for clustering $S$ into $k$ groups.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{Step \arabic*.}, leftmargin=*, itemsep=8pt]
    \item \textbf{Build the affinity matrix.}\\
    Form $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ with $A_{ij} = \exp(-\|s_i - s_j\|^2 / 2\sigma^2)$ for $i \neq j$ and $A_{ii} = 0$.

    \item \textbf{Construct the normalized matrix $L$.}\\
    Compute $D = \mathrm{diag}(\sum_j A_{1j}, \ldots, \sum_j A_{nj})$ and form:
    \[
        L = D^{-1/2} A D^{-1/2}.
    \]

    \item \textbf{Find the $k$ leading eigenvectors of $L$.}\\
    Compute $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_k$, the eigenvectors corresponding to the $k$ largest eigenvalues of $L$ (chosen to be orthogonal in the case of repeated eigenvalues). Form the matrix $X = [x_1 \mid x_2 \mid \cdots \mid x_k] \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times k}$ with the eigenvectors as columns.

    \item \textbf{Row-normalize $X$ to form $Y$.}\\
    Construct the matrix $Y \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times k}$ by normalizing each row of $X$ to unit Euclidean length:
    \begin{equation}
        Y_{ij} = \frac{X_{ij}}{\left(\sum_{j'} X_{ij'}^2\right)^{1/2}}.
        \label{eq:rownorm}
    \end{equation}

    \item \textbf{Cluster the rows of $Y$ with $k$-means.}\\
    Treat each row $y_i \in \mathbb{R}^k$ of $Y$ as a data point and apply $k$-means (or any algorithm minimizing distortion) to obtain $k$ cluster assignments.

    \item \textbf{Transfer assignments back.}\\
    Assign the original point $s_i$ to cluster $j$ if and only if row $i$ of $Y$ was assigned to cluster $j$ in Step 5.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The Role of Each Step}

It is worth pausing to understand intuitively what each step accomplishes.

\paragraph{Step 2 (normalization to form $L$).}
The raw affinity matrix $A$ gives high total weight to vertices with many neighbors (high-degree nodes). Without normalization, the eigenvectors of $A$ would be dominated by degree effects rather than cluster structure. 

\paragraph{Step 3 (eigenvectors).}
The $k$ leading eigenvectors of $L$ span a subspace that is nearly invariant under the random walk dynamics. In the ideal case of perfectly separated clusters, this subspace has a precise block structure (Section~\ref{sec:ideal}). Taking $k$ eigenvectors simultaneously is critical: using fewer eigenvectors discards cluster information; using more mixes in irrelevant dimensions that disrupt the subsequent $k$-means step.

\paragraph{Step 4 (row normalization).}
This step is the key innovation of NJW over earlier methods. The delicacy of this move will be explained in the ideal case of well-separated clusters.

\paragraph{Step 5 ($k$-means).}
$k$-means in $\mathbb{R}^k$ is fast and simple. Because $k \ll n$, this step is computationally cheap. NJW note that initializing the $k$ centroids at approximately $90^\circ$ to each other (exploiting the near-orthogonality of the cluster centroids in $Y$) improves convergence significantly, often requiring only a single pass of $k$-means.

% =============================================================
\section{Analysis: Why the Algorithm Works}
% =============================================================

The theoretical core of NJW is a two-part argument: an ``ideal case'' analysis showing the algorithm is \emph{exact} when clusters are perfectly separated, followed by a perturbation argument showing it remains approximately correct when clusters are nearly separated.

\subsection{The Ideal Case: Disconnected Components}
In the ideal case, the data points form $k$ clusters such that the similarity between points in different clusters is exactly zero ($A_{ij} = 0$ if $s_i, s_j$ are in different clusters). Say there are $k$ clusters of sizes $n_1, n_2,\dots n_k$.

\subsubsection{Block Diagonal Structure}
By reordering the indices, the affinity matrix $A$ (and consequently $L$) becomes block diagonal:
\[ L = \begin{bmatrix} L_1 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ 0 & L_2 & \dots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \dots & L_k \end{bmatrix} \]
where each $L_i$ corresponds to a single cluster $C_i$.

\subsubsection{Eigenvectors as Indicators}
Because $L$ is block diagonal, its eigenvectors are block-structured. Since each $L_i$ is the normalized affinity of a connected subgraph with positive entries, the Perron--Frobenius theorem guarantees that $L_i$ has a unique principal eigenvector $z_i \in \mathbb{R}^{n_i}$ with positive entries and eigenvalue 1. All other eigenvalues of $L_i$ are strictly less than 1. The matrix $\hat{L}$ therefore has eigenvalue 1 with multiplicity $k = 3$, and the corresponding eigenvectors are:
\[ v_1 = [z_1, 0, \dots, 0]^T, \quad v_2 = [0, z_2, \dots, 0]^T, \dots, \quad v_k = [0, 0, \dots, z_k]^T \]
where the $z_i$'s could look like(We dont know)
\[z_i = [0.5, 0.5, 0.5, \dots]^T\] or \[z_i  = [1.234, 1.234, 1.234, \dots]^T\]
The matrix $X$ then looks like:
\[ X = \begin{bmatrix} z_1 & 0 & \dots & 0 \\ 0 & z_2 & \dots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \dots & z_k \end{bmatrix} \]
(Each column is the block eigenvector of one cluster, padded with zeros for the other clusters.)

However, since 1 is a \emph{repeated} eigenvalue of $L$, the eigenvector basis is not unique. Any orthogonal rotation $\hat{X} R$ with $R^\top R = I$ is also a valid eigenvector matrix. This ambiguity is important: we \emph{cannot} interpret individual eigenvectors (columns of $X$) as cluster indicators in general. The stable object is the \emph{subspace} spanned by the columns, not any specific choice of basis.

Therefore NJW apply row normalization (Step 4). After normalizing to unit length, every row corresponding to cluster $i$ becomes the unit vector $e_i^\top$(or, if $\hat{X}$ was replaced by $\hat{X}R$, every such row becomes $e_i^\top R$). This gives:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{Y} = \begin{bmatrix} \hat{Y}^{(1)} \\ \hat{Y}^{(2)} \\ \hat{Y}^{(3)} \end{bmatrix}
    = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{1} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{1} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{1} \end{bmatrix} R,
    \label{eq:Y_ideal}
\end{equation}
where each block $\hat{Y}^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_i \times k}$ has all rows equal to $e_i^\top R$---a single fixed row vector.

In other words: after row normalization, all points from the same cluster are mapped to \emph{exactly the same point} on the unit $(k-1)$-sphere in $\mathbb{R}^k$, and these $k$ points are mutually orthogonal (at $90^\circ$ from each other). $k$-means on $\hat{Y}$ then trivially recovers the true clustering.

The key insight is that row normalization removes the dependence on the arbitrary rotation $R$. Any $R$ leads to the same \emph{geometric} configuration of the rows of $\hat{Y}$: $k$ mutually orthogonal points. The specific rotation does not matter for $k$-means.

\subsection{The General (Perturbed) Case}

In practice, $A$ has nonzero off-diagonal blocks: there are small positive affinities between points in different clusters. Write $A = \hat{A} + E$ where $\hat{A}$ is the ideal block-diagonal matrix and $E$ captures the inter-cluster affinities. Correspondingly, $L = \hat{L} + \tilde{E}$ for some perturbation $\tilde{E}$ (derived from $E$ after the $D^{-1/2}$ normalization).

The central question is: when the perturbation $E$ is small, how close are the rows of $Y$ (from the perturbed $L$) to those of $\hat{Y}$ (from the ideal $\hat{L}$)?

\paragraph{Stability via the eigengap.} Matrix perturbation theory (Davis--Kahan theorem) gives bounds on how much the eigenvector subspace changes under perturbation. Specifically, the subspace spanned by the $k$ leading eigenvectors of $L$ is close to that of $\hat{L}$ if and only if the \textbf{eigengap} $\delta = |\lambda_k - \lambda_{k+1}|$ is large---i.e., the $k$-th and $(k+1)$-th eigenvalues of $\hat{L}$ are well-separated.

From the block structure of $\hat{L}$, the $k$ largest eigenvalues of $\hat{L}$ are all equal to 1 (one per cluster), and $\lambda_{k+1} = \max_i \lambda_2^{(i)}$, the largest second eigenvalue across all intra-cluster blocks. Therefore, the eigengap is:
\begin{equation}
    \delta = |1 - \max_i \lambda_2^{(i)}|.
\end{equation}
A large eigengap means $\lambda_2^{(i)}$ is small for all $i$---which is exactly the condition that the random walk within each cluster mixes rapidly. NJW formalize this as:

\begin{keybox}
\textbf{Assumption A1 (Intra-cluster cohesiveness):} There exists $\delta > 0$ such that $\lambda_2^{(i)} \leq 1 - \delta$ for all $i = 1, \ldots, k$.
\end{keybox}

The second eigenvalue $\lambda_2^{(i)}$ is related to the \emph{Cheeger constant} $h(S_i)$ of cluster $i$, defined as:
\begin{equation}
    h(S_i) = \min_{\mathcal{I} \subseteq \{1,\ldots,n_i\}} \frac{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{I},\, k \notin \mathcal{I}} A_{jk}^{(ii)}}{\min\left\{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{I}} d_j^{(i)},\, \sum_{k \notin \mathcal{I}} d_k^{(i)}\right\}}.
    \label{eq:cheeger}
\end{equation}
This is the minimum normalized cut within cluster $i$ (the hardest bisection of $S_i$ itself). A large Cheeger constant means $S_i$ is hard to split---it is a genuinely tight cluster. Spectral graph theory gives:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{(h(S_i))^2}{2} \leq 1 - \lambda_2^{(i)} \leq 2h(S_i),
\end{equation}
so Assumption A1 holds if and only if all clusters have large Cheeger constants (i.e., none of them is itself nearly bisectable).

\paragraph{Inter-cluster separation conditions.}
Beyond intra-cluster cohesiveness, NJW require two conditions controlling the inter-cluster affinities.

\begin{keybox}
\textbf{Assumption A2 (Small inter-cluster total affinity):} There exists $\epsilon_1 > 0$ such that for all pairs of distinct clusters $i_1 \neq i_2$:
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{j \in S_{i_1}} \sum_{k \in S_{i_2}} \frac{A_{jk}^2}{d_j d_k} \leq \epsilon_1.
    \label{eq:A2}
\end{equation}
\end{keybox}

\begin{keybox}
\textbf{Assumption A3 (Points more connected internally than externally):} There exists $\epsilon_2 > 0$ such that for every cluster $i$ and every point $j \in S_i$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\sum_{k:\, k \notin S_i} A_{jk}}{d_j} \leq \epsilon_2 \left(\sum_{k,\, l \in S_i} \frac{A_{kl}^2}{d_k d_l}\right)^{-1/2}.
    \label{eq:A3}
\end{equation}
\end{keybox}

Assumption A2 says the total normalized inter-cluster affinity is small. Assumption A3 says each point is more strongly connected to its own cluster than to other clusters. Both are natural conditions for well-separated clusters: for clusters of size $O(n)$ with Gaussian affinities decaying with distance, these conditions hold as long as the inter-cluster distances are large relative to $\sigma$.

\begin{keybox}
\textbf{Assumption A4 (No isolated vertices):} There exists $C > 0$ such that for all $i$, $j \in \{1,\ldots,n_i\}$:
\begin{equation}
    d_j^{(i)} \geq \frac{\sum_{k=1}^{n_i} d_k^{(i)}}{C n_i}.
    \label{eq:A4}
\end{equation}
\end{keybox}

This benign condition requires that no vertex within a cluster is excessively weakly connected compared to the cluster average. It prevents degenerate cases where a near-isolated vertex within a cluster distorts the eigenvectors.

\paragraph{The main theorem.}

\begin{theorem}[NJW Theorem 2]
Let Assumptions A1, A2, A3, and A4 hold. Define $\epsilon = \sqrt{k(k-1)\epsilon_1 + k\epsilon_2^2}$. If $\delta > (2 + \sqrt{2})\epsilon$, then there exist $k$ mutually orthogonal unit vectors $r_1, \ldots, r_k$ such that the rows of $Y$ satisfy:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sum_{j=1}^{n_i} \|y_j^{(i)} - r_i\|_2^2
    \leq 4C\left(4 + 2\sqrt{k}\right)^2 \frac{\epsilon^2}{(\delta - \sqrt{2}\epsilon)^2}.
    \label{eq:thm2}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

This theorem says: when the eigengap $\delta$ is large relative to the inter-cluster perturbation $\epsilon$, the rows of $Y$ form tight clusters around $k$ mutually orthogonal points on the unit sphere. The bound \eqref{eq:thm2} quantifies precisely how tight these clusters are. As $\epsilon \to 0$ (clusters become perfectly separated), the right-hand side $\to 0$ and the rows converge to the ideal configuration of Proposition 1.

The condition $\delta > (2 + \sqrt{2})\epsilon$ makes explicit the trade-off: a larger eigengap $\delta$ (more cohesive clusters, better separated) allows the algorithm to tolerate larger perturbations $\epsilon$ (more inter-cluster affinity) while still producing useful clusterings.

\section{Choosing $\sigma^2$ Automatically}
\label{sec:sigma}
% =============================================================

The parameter $\sigma^2$ controls the scale of the Gaussian affinity. Theorem 2 gives an implicit prescription: choose $\sigma^2$ to maximize the eigengap $\delta$, since a larger $\delta$ gives a tighter guarantee.

NJW propose a practical surrogate. For the correct $\sigma^2$, Theorem 2 predicts that the rows of $Y$ form $k$ tight clusters on the unit sphere. The distortion of the $k$-means solution (the total within-cluster variance in $\mathbb{R}^k$) is therefore small. NJW simply search over a grid of $\sigma^2$ values, run the full algorithm for each, and select the $\sigma^2$ that minimizes the $k$-means distortion:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma^{*2} = \arg\min_{\sigma^2}\; \text{(k-means distortion of } Y\text{'s rows)}.
    \label{eq:sigma_search}
\end{equation}
This requires running the algorithm multiple times, but the search is inexpensive because $k$-means in $\mathbb{R}^k$ is fast for small $k$.



\section{Computational cost}
There are three main costs.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em]
    \item \textbf{Graph construction.} Exact pairwise distances would cost $O(n^2d)$, but the stored sparse $k$-NN graph has only $O(mn)$ nonzeros.
    \item \textbf{Eigensolve.} This is the main bottleneck. Dense eigendecomposition is cubic in $n$, while sparse iterative eigensolvers scale much better in practice and depend strongly on the number of graph edges and the spectral gap \cite{vonluxburg2007tutorial,ng2001spectral}.
    \item \textbf{$k$-means in spectral space.} Once the embedding is computed, the final step is cheap because the embedding has only $k$ columns.
\end{enumerate}

We measured these costs directly. On a two-moons benchmark, increasing $n$ from $150$ to $900$ increased the spectral pipeline runtime from about $0.012$ s to $0.220$ s, while the plain $k$-means baseline remained around $0.009$--$0.012$ s. The eigensolver dominated the growth, while graph memory rose from about $24$ KiB to $161$ KiB for the sparse affinity matrix. Figure~\ref{fig:scaling} shows these trends. Exact timings vary by machine, so we interpret the trend rather than the individual seconds.

\section{Experimental design}
\subsection{Evaluation metrics}
For labeled benchmarks we report accuracy after optimal label matching, adjusted Rand index (ARI), normalized mutual information (NMI), and purity.

Because cluster labels are permutation-invariant, accuracy is computed only after solving the best one-to-one assignment between predicted cluster IDs and ground-truth classes:
\[
\mathrm{Acc}(y,\hat y)=\frac{1}{n}\max_{\pi\in S_k}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}\{y_i = \pi(\hat y_i)\}.
\]
Here $S_k$ denotes the set of permutations of the cluster labels.

ARI compares two partitions through pairwise co-membership counts and adjusts for the agreement expected by chance. Using the standard contingency-table notation, it can be written as \cite{hubert1985comparing}
\[
\mathrm{ARI}=
\frac{
\sum_{ij} \binom{n_{ij}}{2} -
\dfrac{\sum_i \binom{a_i}{2} \sum_j \binom{b_j}{2}}{\binom{n}{2}}
}{
\dfrac{1}{2}\left[\sum_i \binom{a_i}{2}+\sum_j \binom{b_j}{2}\right]-
\dfrac{\sum_i \binom{a_i}{2} \sum_j \binom{b_j}{2}}{\binom{n}{2}}
}.
\]
A value near $0$ indicates roughly chance-level agreement, and $1$ indicates identical partitions.

NMI measures the information shared by two partitions:
\[
\mathrm{NMI}(U,V)=\frac{I(U;V)}{\mathrm{avg}\bigl(H(U),H(V)\bigr)},
\]
where $I(U;V)$ is mutual information and $H(\cdot)$ is entropy. The normalized version lies in $[0,1]$ and is invariant to label permutations \cite{sklearnNMI}.

Purity assigns each predicted cluster to its majority true label and reports the resulting fraction of correctly assigned points:
\[
\mathrm{Purity}(\mathcal{C},\mathcal{G}) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{C\in \mathcal{C}} \max_{G\in \mathcal{G}} |C \cap G|.
\]
In the discussion below, ARI is the most informative single metric because it is sensitive to global partition structure rather than only majority assignment.

\subsection{Datasets}
We used the following groups of experiments.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
    \item \textbf{Synthetic success cases:} two moons, concentric circles, Gaussian blobs, high-dimensional moons (moons embedded in 20 dimensions), and high-dimensional blobs.
    \item \textbf{Synthetic stress cases:} overlapping blobs and a bridge-connected two-cluster dataset.
    \item \textbf{Real/vector datasets:} Iris and four digit experiments: $\{0,1\}$, $\{1,7\}$, $\{0,1,3,6\}$, and all ten classes. We use \texttt{sklearn.datasets.load\_digits}, which provides a compact and reproducible benchmark \cite{sklearnDigits}.
    \item \textbf{Graph datasets:} Zachary's karate club and a stochastic block model benchmark.
    \item \textbf{Robustness and scaling:} a Gaussian-noise sweep on two moons, and a runtime/memory benchmark as $n$ increases.
\end{itemize}

For graph datasets, plain feature-space $k$-means is not naturally defined. To keep the comparison explicit, we used a weak but transparent baseline: $k$-means on adjacency rows. This is not a state-of-the-art graph clustering baseline, but it provides a concrete comparator.

The bridge-connected example is also a useful reminder that a visually plausible 2D partition need not yield a high pairwise clustering score. Comparing the three panels in Figure~\ref{fig:failure2}, the two endpoint masses look separable at a glance, but the bridge region generates many ambiguous labels for both algorithms. Because ARI evaluates agreement at the level of point pairs rather than only the gross shape of the partition, errors on a relatively small set of bridge points can induce a much larger number of pairwise disagreements. This is exactly the kind of failure we mean in this report: not random labeling everywhere, but a structurally important mismatch between the graph-induced cut and the intended classes.

\section{Results: real data and digit subsets}
We study four digit subsets of increasing difficulty: $\{0,1\}$, $\{1,7\}$, $\{0,1,3,6\}$, and all digits. This progression makes it possible to distinguish regimes where local graph structure is helpful from regimes where raw pixel-space geometry already favors centroid-based clustering. Table~\ref{tab:digits} reports those results. The outcome is deliberately presented as a mixed result rather than a blanket win for spectral clustering. Spectral clustering is excellent on $\{0,1\}$ and strong on all ten digits, but it is slightly worse than $k$-means on $\{1,7\}$ and clearly worse on $\{0,1,3,6\}$ for the particular $k$-NN graph used here. Our hypothesis is that raw pixel-space Euclidean distances already separate some digit subsets nearly linearly, while the local graph can become brittle when classes have different stroke styles and densities.

\section{Additional experiments}

\subsection{Parameter sensitivity map}
Spectral clustering depends strongly on the graph it constructs. Figure~\ref{fig:paramsensitivity} shows a phase-diagram style sweep on two moons, varying both the feature noise and the number of nearest neighbors in the graph. The final red-outlined column reports the $k$-means baseline at the same noise level. The main pattern is useful: there is a broad stable band at moderate neighbor counts, but too few neighbors can fragment the graph, while too many neighbors can wash out the two-moon geometry once noise is substantial. In the high-neighbor regime, performance often approaches the $k$-means baseline, consistent with the idea that an overly dense neighborhood graph loses the local manifold structure that gives spectral clustering its advantage.

\subsection{Eigengap study}
A common heuristic for spectral clustering is that the first few eigenvalues of the graph Laplacian reveal the number and strength of clusters. Figure~\ref{fig:eigengap} compares the smallest eigenvalues on several datasets. The stochastic block model has a clear gap after the third eigenvalue and is perfectly recovered, which matches the textbook story. The two-moons case also has useful low-eigenvalue structure. But the overlapping blobs and digit cases are less clean: a visible gap alone is not a complete certificate of good clustering. This experiment makes the eigengap heuristic concrete while also showing its limitation.

    \caption{Smallest eigenvalues of the symmetric normalized graph Laplacian. A clean eigengap often correlates with clusterability, but it is not by itself a guarantee of high clustering accuracy.}
    \label{fig:eigengap}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Graph construction ablation}
The strongest additional lesson is that graph construction is the main inductive bias. We compare three affinity constructions:
\[
W_{ij}^{\mathrm{dense}} = \exp(-\gamma \lVert x_i-x_j\rVert_2^2),
\]
\[
W_{ij}^{\mathrm{kNN}} = \mathbf{1}\{j\in \mathcal{N}_m(i) \text{ or } i\in \mathcal{N}_m(j)\} \exp(-\gamma \lVert x_i-x_j\rVert_2^2),
\]
and
\[
W_{ij}^{\mathrm{mutual}} = \mathbf{1}\{j\in \mathcal{N}_m(i) \text{ and } i\in \mathcal{N}_m(j)\} \exp(-\gamma \lVert x_i-x_j\rVert_2^2).
\]
Here $\mathcal{N}_m(i)$ denotes the directed $m$-nearest-neighbor set of point $i$. Figure~\ref{fig:graphablation} shows that the same spectral clustering pipeline can behave very differently depending on this choice. On moons and circles, local sparse graphs preserve manifold geometry and dense RBF does poorly. On the digit subset $\{0,1,3,6\}$, dense RBF performs well, while mutual-$k$-NN becomes too restrictive and loses useful edges. The conclusion is not that one graph is universally best; rather, graph construction must match the data geometry.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.70\textwidth]{final_pic/graph_construction_ablation.png}
    \caption{Graph construction ablation. The same spectral clustering pipeline can behave very differently depending on whether the graph is dense, local sparse, or mutual local.}
    \label{fig:graphablation}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Graph fragility archetypes}
Figure~\ref{fig:failuretaxonomy} collects three interpretable fragility modes. First, too few neighbors can fragment a manifold graph. Second, a long thin bridge of intermediate points can remove the clean low-density separation between two masses. Third, varying density and imbalance can make a single global neighborhood scale inappropriate. The point of this figure is not that spectral clustering always fails on such inputs; rather, it illustrates how fixed graph parameters can become mismatched to the local geometry of the data.

    \caption{Three graph-fragility archetypes in original 2D coordinates $(x,y)$: graph fragmentation, a long thin bridge, and varying-density geometry.}
    \label{fig:failuretaxonomy}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Runtime bottleneck breakdown}
Figure~\ref{fig:bottleneck} expands the computational scaling analysis by decomposing spectral clustering runtime into graph construction, eigensolver, and final $k$-means-on-embedding stages, and overlays the plain $k$-means baseline from the same benchmark. For the largest sizes tested, the spectral total almost exactly tracks eigensolver time, while the baseline remains much smaller. This agrees with the theoretical discussion: once a sparse graph has been built, the expensive stage is extracting the relevant eigenvectors of a large sparse Laplacian.

\section{Discussion}
Overall, the experiments match the standard theory quite well.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
    \item On non-convex synthetic datasets, spectral clustering substantially outperforms $k$-means.
    \item On standard blob-like data, both methods are strong and $k$-means can even be slightly better.
    \item On graph/community datasets, spectral clustering is especially natural and strong.
    \item On the digit subsets, spectral clustering is not uniformly better; it ties on $\{0,1\}$, is nearly perfect but slightly worse on $\{1,7\}$, loses on $\{0,1,3,6\}$, and wins clearly on all ten digits. This is a useful reminder that graph construction is a modeling choice, not a free lunch.
\end{itemize}

The main surprises are the $\{1,7\}$ and $\{0,1,3,6\}$ digit subsets, where $k$-means already aligns well with raw pixel geometry and our particular neighborhood graph is comparatively poor. The high-dimensional moons and bridge examples also show that a numerical advantage can be visually modest. These are informative negative results rather than embarrassments: they expose exactly where normalized spectral clustering depends on a good graph model.
