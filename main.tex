\section{Introduction}
Spectral clustering is a graph-based clustering method that is especially attractive when cluster geometry is non-convex, curved, or better described by local neighborhood relations than by Euclidean centroids. The basic move is to convert data into a weighted graph, study the graph Laplacian, extract a small number of eigenvectors, and run a simple clustering method in that spectral embedding. We focus on the normalized spectral clustering algorithm of Ng, Jordan, and Weiss (NJW) \cite{ng2001spectral}, while relating it to the normalized-cut objective of Shi and Malik \cite{shi2000normalized} and the broader graph-Laplacian view in von Luxburg's tutorial \cite{vonluxburg2007tutorial}.

The project brief asks for three components: a theory summary, an algorithmic description including computational cost, and a data exploration section with synthetic and real datasets, comparisons with $k$-means, and noise robustness. This report follows that structure closely.

\section{Theory}
\subsection{Graph construction and Laplacians}
Given data points $x_1,\dots,x_n \in \mathbb{R}^d$, we build a weighted undirected graph with affinity matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$, where $W_{ij}$ is large when $x_i$ and $x_j$ are similar. Let $D$ be the diagonal degree matrix, $D_{ii} = \sum_j W_{ij}$. The two most common Laplacians are the unnormalized Laplacian
\[
L = D - W
\]
and the symmetric normalized Laplacian
\[
L_{\mathrm{sym}} = I - D^{-1/2} W D^{-1/2}.
\]
When the graph has $k$ connected components, $L_{\mathrm{sym}}$ has exactly $k$ zero eigenvalues, and the associated eigenvectors are piecewise constant on those components. In the nearly-disconnected case, the bottom $k$ eigenvectors remain approximately piecewise constant, which is the spectral signal exploited by clustering algorithms \cite{vonluxburg2007tutorial}.

\subsection{From normalized cut to an eigenproblem}
For a two-way partition $(A, \bar A)$, the cut is
\[
\mathrm{cut}(A,\bar A) = \sum_{i\in A,\,j\in \bar A} W_{ij}.
\]
Minimizing cut alone is degenerate: it prefers isolating tiny sets. Shi and Malik proposed the normalized cut objective
\[
\mathrm{Ncut}(A,\bar A)
=
\frac{\mathrm{cut}(A,\bar A)}{\mathrm{vol}(A)}
+
\frac{\mathrm{cut}(A,\bar A)}{\mathrm{vol}(\bar A)},
\qquad
\mathrm{vol}(A)=\sum_{i\in A} D_{ii},
\]
which balances low inter-cluster connectivity against cluster size \cite{shi2000normalized}. The exact optimization is discrete and NP-hard in general, so spectral clustering uses a continuous relaxation. That relaxation leads to a generalized eigenvalue problem and, after normalization, to the bottom eigenvectors of a Laplacian matrix. This is the main mathematical reason spectral clustering works: difficult discrete partitioning is replaced by a tractable linear algebra problem \cite{shi2000normalized,vonluxburg2007tutorial}.

\subsection{Why NJW?}
There are several common ``flavors'' of spectral clustering. Unnormalized methods work directly with $L=D-W$. Shi--Malik normalized cuts solve a generalized eigenproblem. NJW instead computes the top eigenvectors of a normalized affinity-like matrix, or equivalently the bottom eigenvectors of a normalized Laplacian, then normalizes rows and applies $k$-means \cite{ng2001spectral}. We chose NJW because it is simple, standard, easy to implement in Python, and closely tied to the normalized-cut relaxation while remaining computationally clean.

\section{Algorithm}
\subsection{Implemented method}
Our implementation uses an $m$-nearest-neighbor graph with RBF weights:
\[
W_{ij} = \exp\!\bigl(-\gamma \lVert x_i-x_j\rVert_2^2\bigr)
\]
when $x_j$ is among the $m$ nearest neighbors of $x_i$. The scale parameter $\gamma$ is chosen from the median nonzero neighbor distance. The graph is then symmetrized.

\paragraph{Input.} Data matrix $X\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d}$, number of clusters $k$, number of neighbors $m$.

\paragraph{Output.} Predicted cluster labels $\hat y_1,\dots,\hat y_n$.

\paragraph{Steps.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em]
    \item Build a sparse weighted $m$-nearest-neighbor graph.
    \item Form $L_{\mathrm{sym}} = I-D^{-1/2}WD^{-1/2}$.
    \item Compute the $k$ eigenvectors associated with the smallest eigenvalues of $L_{\mathrm{sym}}$.
    \item Stack them into $U\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times k}$ and row-normalize to obtain $Y$.
    \item Run $k$-means on the rows of $Y$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Computational cost}
There are three main costs.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em]
    \item \textbf{Graph construction.} Exact pairwise distances would cost $O(n^2d)$, but our implementation uses a sparse $k$-NN graph so the stored graph has only $O(mn)$ nonzeros.
    \item \textbf{Eigensolve.} This is the main bottleneck. Dense eigendecomposition is cubic in $n$, but sparse iterative eigensolvers scale much better in practice and depend strongly on the number of graph edges and the spectral gap \cite{vonluxburg2007tutorial,ng2001spectral}.
    \item \textbf{$k$-means in spectral space.} Once the embedding is computed, the final step is cheap because the embedding has only $k$ columns.
\end{enumerate}

We measured these costs directly. On a two-moons benchmark, increasing $n$ from $150$ to $900$ increased the spectral pipeline runtime from about $0.012$ s to $0.220$ s, while the $k$-means-only baseline remained around $0.009$--$0.012$ s. The eigensolver dominated the growth, while graph memory rose from about $24$ KiB to $161$ KiB for the sparse affinity matrix. Figure~\ref{fig:scaling} shows these trends. Exact timings vary by machine, so we interpret the trend rather than the individual seconds.

\section{Experimental design}
\subsection{Metrics}
For labeled benchmarks we report accuracy after optimal label matching, adjusted Rand index (ARI), normalized mutual information (NMI), and purity. Purity is especially relevant for the digit-subset experiments because the project brief explicitly asks how close clusters are to being label-pure.

\subsection{Datasets}
We used the following groups of experiments.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
    \item \textbf{Synthetic success cases:} two moons, concentric circles, Gaussian blobs, high-dimensional moons (moons embedded in 20 dimensions), and high-dimensional blobs.
    \item \textbf{Synthetic failure/stress cases:} overlapping blobs and a bridge-connected two-cluster dataset.
    \item \textbf{Real/vector datasets:} Iris and four digits experiments: $\{0,1\}$, $\{1,7\}$, $\{0,1,3,6\}$, and all ten classes. This exactly matches the digit-subset structure requested in the brief, except that we use \texttt{sklearn.datasets.load\_digits} rather than MNIST for speed and reproducibility \cite{sklearnDigits}.
    \item \textbf{Graph datasets:} Zachary's karate club and a stochastic block model benchmark.
    \item \textbf{Robustness and scaling:} a Gaussian-noise sweep on two moons, and a runtime/memory benchmark as $n$ increases.
\end{itemize}

For graph datasets, plain feature-space $k$-means is not naturally defined. To keep the comparison explicit, we used a weak but transparent baseline: $k$-means on adjacency rows. This is not a state-of-the-art graph clustering baseline, but it provides a concrete comparator.

\section{Results: real data and digit subsets}
The project brief explicitly asks for the digit-subset progression $\{0,1\}$, $\{1,7\}$, $\{0,1,3,6\}$, and all digits. Table~\ref{tab:digits} reports those results. The outcome is deliberately presented as a mixed result rather than a blanket win for spectral clustering. Spectral clustering is excellent on $\{0,1\}$ and strong on all ten digits, but it performs poorly on $\{1,7\}$ and underperforms $k$-means on $\{0,1,3,6\}$ for the particular $k$-NN graph used here. Our hypothesis is that raw pixel-space Euclidean distances already separate some digit subsets nearly linearly, while the local graph can become brittle when classes have different stroke styles and densities. This is a useful negative result: spectral clustering is sensitive to graph construction, not a free improvement over $k$-means.

\section{Results: graph data}
Spectral clustering is especially natural on graph data. On Zachary's karate club graph, spectral clustering reaches accuracy $0.971$ and ARI $0.882$, far above the adjacency-row $k$-means baseline (accuracy $0.676$, ARI $0.109$). On a planted stochastic block model, spectral clustering perfectly recovers the communities, while adjacency-row $k$-means is also strong but slightly worse. These two experiments strengthen the ``community detection'' side of the project brief.

\section{Noise robustness and computational scaling}
Figure~\ref{fig:noise} shows ARI under additive Gaussian noise on two moons. Spectral clustering is excellent at low noise, but performance becomes unstable once noise disrupts the local graph structure. The curve is not monotone: the small bump around intermediate noise is likely seed-level variation from a single trial, and should not be over-interpreted. The robust conclusion is that the method has a low-noise success regime and then rapidly becomes graph-construction sensitive.

\subsection{Parameter sensitivity map}
Spectral clustering depends strongly on the graph it constructs.  Figure~\ref{fig:paramsensitivity} shows a phase-diagram style sweep on two moons, varying both the feature noise and the number of nearest neighbors in the graph.  The main pattern is useful: there is a broad stable band at moderate neighbor counts, but too few neighbors can fragment the graph, while too many neighbors can wash out the two-moon geometry once noise is substantial.  This is a more informative robustness test than a single noise curve because it shows that ``spectral clustering'' is really a family of graph choices, not a parameter-free method.

\subsection{Eigengap study}
A common heuristic for spectral clustering is that the first few eigenvalues of the graph Laplacian reveal the number and strength of clusters.  Figure~\ref{fig:eigengap} compares the smallest eigenvalues on several datasets.  The stochastic block model has a clear gap after the third eigenvalue and is perfectly recovered, which matches the textbook story.  The two-moons case also has useful low-eigenvalue structure.  But the overlapping blobs and digit cases are less clean: a visible gap alone is not a complete certificate of good clustering.  This experiment makes the eigengap heuristic concrete while also showing its limitation.

    \caption{Smallest eigenvalues of the symmetric normalized graph Laplacian.  A clean eigengap often correlates with clusterability, but it is not by itself a guarantee of high clustering accuracy.}

\end{figure}

\subsection{Graph construction ablation}
The strongest additional lesson is that graph construction is the main inductive bias.  Figure~\ref{fig:graphablation} compares a symmetrized $k$-NN RBF graph, a mutual-$k$-NN RBF graph, and a dense RBF graph.  On moons and circles, local sparse graphs preserve manifold geometry and dense RBF does poorly.  On the digit subset $\{0,1,3,6\}$, dense RBF performs well, while mutual-$k$-NN becomes too restrictive and loses useful edges.  The conclusion is not that one graph is universally best; rather, graph construction must match the data geometry.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.70\textwidth]{final_pic/graph_construction_ablation.png}
    \caption{Graph construction ablation.  The same spectral clustering pipeline can behave very differently depending on whether the graph is sparse local, mutual local, or dense.}
    \label{fig:graphablation}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Failure mode taxonomy}
Figure~\ref{fig:failuretaxonomy} collects three interpretable failure or stress modes.  First, too few neighbors can fragment a manifold graph.  Second, a bridge of intermediate points can remove the clean cut between two clusters.  Third, varying density and imbalance can make a single global neighborhood scale inappropriate.  The third example is not a catastrophic failure in this particular run, but it illustrates why fixed graph parameters are fragile: the same $k$-NN rule means different things in dense and diffuse regions.

    \caption{Three failure archetypes for graph-based clustering: graph fragmentation, bridge connections, and varying-density geometry.}

\end{figure}

\subsection{Runtime bottleneck breakdown}
Figure~\ref{fig:bottleneck} expands the computational scaling analysis by decomposing spectral clustering runtime into graph construction, eigensolver, and final $k$-means-on-embedding stages, and overlays the plain $k$-means baseline from the same benchmark.  For the largest sizes tested, the spectral total almost exactly tracks eigensolver time, while the baseline remains much smaller.  This agrees with the theoretical discussion: once a sparse graph has been built, the expensive stage is extracting the relevant eigenvectors of a large sparse Laplacian.

\section{Discussion}
Overall, the experiments match the standard theory quite well.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
    \item On non-convex synthetic datasets, spectral clustering substantially outperforms $k$-means.
    \item On standard blob-like data, both methods are strong and $k$-means can even be slightly better.
    \item On graph/community datasets, spectral clustering is especially natural and strong.
    \item On the required digit subsets, spectral clustering is not uniformly better; it wins on $\{0,1\}$ and all digits, but loses on $\{1,7\}$ and $\{0,1,3,6\}$ under the chosen graph. This is a useful reminder that graph construction is a modeling choice, not a free lunch.
\end{itemize}

The project brief also asks whether the results match expectations. The answer is mostly yes, but the exceptions are important. The main surprises are the $\{1,7\}$ and $\{0,1,3,6\}$ digit subsets, where $k$-means already aligns well with raw pixel geometry and our particular neighborhood graph is comparatively poor. The high-dimensional moons and bridge examples also show that a numerical advantage can be visually modest. These are informative negative results, so the revised captions deliberately avoid overstating what the plots show.
